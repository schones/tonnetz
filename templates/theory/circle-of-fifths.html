What is the Circle of Fifths?

The circle arranges all 12 keys by how closely related they are. Neighboring keys share 6 out of 7 notes — they're nearly identical. Keys across the circle share almost nothing.

Click any key to hear its scale and see which chords belong to it. The highlighted arc is your diatonic window.

The Diatonic Window

The highlighted arc shows the 7 diatonic chords of the selected key — the chords that naturally belong there.

  • Move one step clockwise = sharpen one note. The arc slides over.
  • Move one step counter-clockwise = flatten one note.
  • Major chords (outer ring) and their relative minors (inner ring) always pair up at the same position.

You'll notice only 6 chords are highlighted on the rings. The 7th — vii° — is a diminished chord, which doesn't fit on either the major or minor ring. Look for the small dashed marker between the rings at the edge of the arc. Diminished chords are inherently unstable: vii° almost always wants to resolve back to I.

Try It

Close neighbors: Click C, then G, then D. Notice how the arc slides one position each time — you're moving through closely related keys.
Distant keys: Click C, then F♯. The arcs barely overlap — these keys share almost no common chords.
Relative pairs: Click the outer C, then the inner A. Same arc position, same notes — C major and A minor are relative keys.
