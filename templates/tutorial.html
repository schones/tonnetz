2

Follow the guide

When you open a song, the left sidebar walks you through it step by step. Each step highlights a chord and explains what's happening musically. Use the Next and Back buttons, or click any step to jump around.

The progress bar at the top shows how far through the walkthrough you are. The teaching text at the bottom is the most important part — read it!
3

See harmony in space

The main panel shows the Tonnetz — a map where every chord is a triangle and nearby triangles share notes. When Norwegian Wood moves from G major to F major, watch how close they are on the grid. That closeness is why the move sounds natural.

Tap the ⓘ Learn pill next to "Tonnetz Neighborhood" to read more about how the Tonnetz works.
Try Norwegian Wood →
4

Another way to see it

Click the "Chord wheel" tab to see the same harmony on the Circle of Fifths — the traditional way musicians visualize key relationships. The Tonnetz shows you geometric closeness; the wheel shows you functional relationships.

Tap the ⓘ Learn pill next to the Chord wheel tab to learn about the Circle of Fifths.
5

One note at a time

The P, R, and L buttons in the controls bar are transforms — they flip exactly one note to move from one chord to the next. In Norwegian Wood, the move from G to F isn't a standard key change — it's a modal borrowing. The Tonnetz makes moves like this visible.

Tap ⓘ next to "Transform" to learn what P, R, and L do. These are the building blocks of voice leading — the art of moving between chords as smoothly as possible.
6

Practice what you heard

Each walkthrough step shows "Try this" game pills when there's a relevant exercise. Norwegian Wood's Mixolydian mode connects to the Scale Builder — you can build the Mixolydian scale yourself and hear how it differs from regular major.

Game pills link directly into the game with the right key and mode pre-loaded. You don't have to configure anything.
Try Scale Builder →
7

Feel the groove

Click the Rhythm tab to see the song's beat pattern. Norwegian Wood is in 6/8 — six beats per bar instead of the usual eight. That's what gives it the gentle rocking waltz feel. Hit the play button to hear it, and use the tempo slider to slow it down.

Every song has rhythm data — even a standard 4/4 backbeat is worth understanding. The Rhythm tab shows time signature, BPM, feel, and a visual breakdown of the beat.
8

Play along

The keyboard at the bottom isn't just for display — click any key to play notes over the running rhythm. Hold Shift for sustain. Use the Rhythm and Chords volume sliders to balance the mix.

In Chord mode, clicking a white key plays the full chord built on that note. Switch to Note mode to play individual notes — great for picking out melodies.
9

Take it to the lab

Hit the Record button in the bottom bar, click through some chords, then press → SkratchLab. Your chord progression exports as building blocks, and the rhythm pattern pre-loads in the Rhythm Builder grid.

SkratchLab is where you build — drag blocks to create chord progressions, add drum patterns, and experiment with your own arrangements.
Open SkratchLab →
